# From Chaos to Crisis: Using Milhazes Freitas' Dynamical-Systems Extreme Value Theory in Econometrics

## Introduction: crises as recurrence, not just shocks

Econometrics usually studies crises through shocks, residuals, volatility bursts, breaks, and regime changes. Those are important objects. But crisis analysis often needs a different question: not only how large an event is, but how the system returns to dangerous regions and how long it stays near them.

That shift matters because much of the social damage in macro-financial episodes does not come from one isolated exceedance. It comes from recurrence and clustering. A single inflation surprise, one day of market stress, or one quarter of weak activity may be manageable. A repeated sequence of extremes changes expectations, credit conditions, policy constraints, and the distribution of future risk.

This is where the rare-event framework associated with Jorge Milhazes Freitas and collaborators becomes useful. It gives us a mathematical language for hitting times, return times, recurrence, clustering of extremes, and multivariate stress. The point is not to claim that economies are deterministic chaotic systems. The point is narrower and more defensible: economic observables can be treated as outputs of an evolving system, and the recurrence structure of rare events can be measured empirically.

## Why standard econometrics is useful but incomplete for tail recurrence

Standard econometrics remains indispensable. Autoregressions describe persistence. GARCH-type models capture volatility clustering. Structural-break models identify regime changes. State-space methods and factor models help summarize latent macro-financial conditions. None of that should be discarded.

The limitation is different. Much of standard practice is organized around conditional means, variances, and residual behavior. Even when tails are modeled explicitly, crisis episodes are often treated as large observations generated by a stochastic process with dependence. That is useful, but it can miss a central issue: whether extremes arrive as isolated observations or as repeated returns to a fragile region.

For policy and risk management, this distinction is first-order. A regulator, treasury, or central bank may care less about one exceedance than about the probability that exceedances cluster over a short horizon. A model with the same marginal tail thickness but a different recurrence structure implies a different practical notion of danger.

## Milhazes Freitas' rare-event framework

The dynamical-systems perspective starts from a simple idea. A system evolves according to some rule, and an observable is measured along its trajectory. In notation,

```text
X_t = phi(f^t(x))
```

where `f` is the evolution rule, `x` is the initial condition, and `phi` is the observable. In an economic context, `phi` might be inflation, unemployment, a sovereign spread, market volatility, or a stress index.

This viewpoint does not require us to know the full structure of the economy. It only requires us to treat the observed time series as the empirical trace of an evolving system. Once that step is taken, rare events can be studied through recurrence:

- a hitting time is the time until the observable enters a crisis region;
- a return time is the time between repeated visits;
- clustering measures whether extremes bunch together rather than arrive independently;
- multivariate tail dependence studies whether stress appears jointly across observables.

Milhazes Freitas' contribution is to provide rigorous tools for these objects. For econometrics, the main value is not metaphysical. It is operational.

## Translation from dynamical systems to econometrics

The translation into applied econometrics is direct:

| Dynamical systems | Econometrics |
|---|---|
| State space | Latent macro-financial configuration |
| Orbit | Historical time path |
| Observable | Measured economic variable |
| Rare set | Crisis or stress region |
| Hitting time | Time until crisis |
| Return time | Time between crises |
| Extremal index | Degree of clustering of extremes |
| Joint recurrence | Multivariate macro-financial stress |

This translation is modest by design. It does not assert that the economy obeys a low-dimensional deterministic law. It says that the statistical structure of recurrence can still be studied as if observed variables were generated by a system moving through more and less dangerous regions.

## Extremal index and why clustering matters

Among the imported concepts, the extremal index is especially useful. Informally, it measures whether extremes tend to arrive one by one or in clusters. When the extremal index is close to one, exceedances behave more like isolated events. When it is materially below one, extremes cluster.

That distinction matters for macro-financial policy because clustering is often what turns a disturbance into a crisis episode. An isolated stress observation may be absorbed. A cluster of stress observations can trigger refinancing problems, fire sales, unemployment persistence, inflation expectations, or political pressure for abrupt intervention.

The extremal index therefore works as a compact diagnostic. It does not replace a full model of the economy. But it tells us something many standard summaries do not: whether the tail is episodic or persistent in time.

## Simulated examples

The repository implements a set of controlled generators precisely to make this distinction concrete. It includes iid Gaussian and Student-t baselines, an AR(1), a GARCH(1,1), a logistic map, a Pomeau-Manneville intermittent map, and coupled logistic maps for multivariate stress.

That mix is important because it allows comparisons across mechanisms. Some processes generate dependence through familiar econometric channels. Others generate recurrence and clustering through nonlinear deterministic dynamics or intermittency. The point of the simulations is not to declare one class true and the other false. It is to show that similar-looking series can have very different rare-event structure.

The repo's simulation and diagnostics notebooks are built around this comparison. They produce:

- exceedance plots under high thresholds;
- runs-based extremal-index comparisons;
- return-time distributions;
- threshold-sensitivity curves;
- multivariate joint-tail summaries.

The core lesson is practical. Tail behavior is not only about how large the extremes are. It is also about how often the process comes back.

## Econometric case study

The repo also includes a macro-financial case-study workflow. The data loaders support free-source inputs through standardized `date`, `series_id`, `value` output, including local CSV contracts and optional connectors for FRED and market proxies. The notebooks can run with synthetic fallback data, but the intended use is empirical application to real macro-financial observables.

A sensible first case study is a stress proxy such as inflation pressure, unemployment jumps, sovereign-spread stress, or market volatility. The workflow is straightforward:

1. define a high threshold for a crisis or stress region;
2. extract exceedances;
3. compute hitting and return times;
4. estimate declustered extremes and extremal index;
5. test sensitivity across thresholds;
6. inspect multivariate joint stress where relevant.

What this adds to standard analysis is not a new theory of everything. It adds a disciplined way to ask whether a variable merely spikes or repeatedly returns to dangerous territory.

## Policy interpretation

This perspective changes the policy question. A central bank or finance ministry may not only want to reduce average inflation, unemployment, or spreads. It may also want to reduce the recurrence and clustering of extreme outcomes.

That is a different policy metric. Two regimes with similar means and variances may differ substantially in crisis recurrence. One may produce rare but isolated stress. Another may produce shorter average return times and more clustered exceedances. The second regime is socially more costly even if conventional averages look similar.

This is why recurrence is politically relevant. It speaks directly to whether policy dampens repeated visits to harmful states rather than merely smoothing the center of the distribution.

## Limitations

The limitations should be stated clearly.

First, none of this proves that economies are deterministic chaotic systems. That claim is stronger than the evidence supports and is unnecessary for the empirical program here.

Second, the rare-event framework does not predict crises perfectly. It provides diagnostics for recurrence and clustering; it is not an oracle.

Third, the mathematical theory does not transfer automatically from stylized dynamical systems to macroeconomic data. Real data have non-stationarity, structural breaks, policy interventions, measurement error, and short samples in the tail.

Fourth, threshold choice matters. That is why the repository explicitly includes threshold-sensitivity analysis rather than pretending there is one canonical crisis cutoff.

Finally, standard econometrics is not being displaced. It remains the benchmark. The claim is only that tail recurrence deserves its own set of tools.

## Conclusion

Econometric crisis analysis should not only ask how large shocks are. It should ask how the system returns to crisis regions. That is the central contribution of bringing Milhazes Freitas' rare-event framework into applied econometrics.

The payoff is not rhetorical. It is analytic. Hitting times, return times, clustering diagnostics, extremal index estimates, and multivariate stress measures give researchers a richer language for studying crisis episodes. They let us distinguish isolated extremes from repeated tail occupation. In practice, that distinction is often more important than one more decimal place on a mean forecast.

The repository behind this article is designed as a bridge: rigorous enough to support a future formal paper, but practical enough to structure a clear computational research program today. The broader argument is simple. Standard econometrics tells us a great deal about dependence. Dynamical-systems extreme value theory helps us ask a sharper question about danger: not only how big the shock is, but how often the system comes back.
